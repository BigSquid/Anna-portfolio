01

Overview

Les Mills International is a global fitness company behind 16 signature group fitness programs (BODYPUMP, BODYCOMBAT, LES MILLS SPRINT & more) delivered in 20,000+ clubs worldwide. Instructors are the lifeblood of this business: certified, passionate fitness professionals who teach quarterly-updated releases to club members.

The Instructor Experience App was a ground-up redesign of the company's primary instructor-facing digital product, unifying four disconnected platforms into a single, cohesive tool and fundamentally changing how instructors learn, prepare, and teach.

The macro user problem

Instructors struggle with unsustainable prep-to-teaching ratios: mastering quarterly content demands excessive time unaligned with earnings.

Every quarter, Les Mills releases new content for each of its 16 programs. Instructors — most of whom teach part-time alongside day jobs — were expected to learn entirely new choreography, build class playlists, and master updated coaching cues. The time demands were eroding both instructor satisfaction and certification rates.

03

My Role & Design Approach

As Lead Product Designer I was both the hands-on designer and the design lead for this initiative — owning the end-to-end experience across all digital touchpoints. This meant:

  • Leading user research, journey mapping, and opportunity framing with the product trio (PM + tech lead)
  • Designing and prototyping the full app experience: information architecture, interaction design, and visual design
  • Running usability testing and assumption validation studies
  • Partnering with engineering on feasibility, particularly around the AI/ML components of Smart Suggestions
  • Navigating significant org complexity: designers reported to product directors rather than me, requiring stakeholder alignment work beyond typical IC scope
→

The org context matters for this case study: I was operating in a player-coach role without direct reports, in an org with limited product and design maturity. A significant part of the design work was also the upstream work of establishing the right conditions for good design — aligning on process, building in research, and creating shared understanding through artifacts like E2E journey maps.

04

End-to-End Solution

Rather than redesigning the existing Releases App in isolation, we used the E2E instructor journey as our north star — mapping all touchpoints by frequency (weekly, quarterly, bi-annually) and identifying where friction was highest. This surfaced that the highest-frequency activities (learn, prep, teach) lived in the worst product (the aging Releases App).

The new Instructor App was built around four core areas:

Learn
Masterclass

Hub for focused, immersive learning. Kickstart your learning journey with digital choreo notes, move glossaries, and coaching cues — all synced to track-level video.

Prep
Playlists

Build, organize, and optimize your class with AI-powered smart suggestions of your most-loved tracks. Replaces entirely manual, multi-PDF process.

Teach
Studio

Studio set-up ready for go time. In-class teaching mode with real-time access to choreo notes, track focus, and session controls.

Develop
Training

Destination for skill-building and professional development. Refine teaching, master new techniques.

05

Case Study 1: Aligning IA to Instructor Expectations

The usability problem

The Masterclass feature gave instructors access to three content types for each release: choreo notes, the Masterclass video, and playlist/audio-only mode. A seemingly simple three-tab structure hid a critical IA problem: instructors consistently landed in the wrong content type, or couldn't find what they needed intuitively.

Three approaches considered

A
Show/hide video only
Simplest change, lowest risk
B
Direct to playlist
Route users immediately to audio-first prep
C
Reset tab expectations Chosen
Redesign the tab structure and its mental model entirely

Decision & rationale

We chose option C — a combined solution: tab structure update plus show/hide video toggle. The reasoning: options A and B treated symptoms. The real issue was that the existing tab structure didn't match how instructors thought about learning modes. Resetting expectations through a clearer IA cost minimal additional dev effort but had substantially higher long-term usability payoff.

This was validated through usability testing, where we confirmed that the revised tab labeling and entry state significantly reduced navigation errors and time-to-task on first use.

06

Case Study 2: Discovering Smart Suggestions

The user problem

Instructors struggle to efficiently create balanced, varied class playlists from past releases due to a lack of search and organizational tools. This manual, time-consuming process often leads to repetitive classes, reducing member engagement and satisfaction.

Creating a mixed playlist required instructors to manually flip through old PDFs, remember which tracks they'd used recently, and manually sequence a class. 100% of research participants reported losing time to this process — average monthly time spent was 7.5 hours.

How the feature evolved through assumption validation

1
Manual usage tracking

Initial assumption: instructors want to track which tracks they've used. Validation showed this was too much manual overhead — instructors wanted the system to do this work for them.

2
Triangulate schedule + tracks

We explored using teaching schedules cross-referenced with track history to surface gaps. Technically complex and required data we didn't yet have at scale.

3
Ask for higher content frequency Final direction

Surface: instructors wanted content refreshed more frequently than quarterly. This was a product/business constraint — but naming it opened up the smart suggestion framing: surface variety within what exists.

The final Smart Suggestions feature used AI-driven recommendations to propose balanced, varied playlists based on instructor history and class-building best practices — surfacing the right tracks at the right time without requiring manual input.

Digital choreo notes: eliminating the PDF

A parallel high-impact feature: replacing printed PDF choreo notes with a fully digital, track-synced experience.

08

Lessons Learned

E2E journey mapping as a strategic tool

Mapping the full instructor journey — not just the Releases App touchpoints — was the single most important early decision. It reframed the scope from "improve an app" to "redesign a system" and provided shared language for prioritization conversations with stakeholders who had conflicting opinions on where to start.

Assumption validation as a design method, not just a testing method

The Smart Suggestions feature is the clearest example: we had three different hypotheses about what instructors wanted, and each one was partially right. Building small validation experiments — rather than committing to a full build — allowed us to triangulate toward a solution that was meaningfully better than any of the individual hypotheses.

Product trio discovery yields better outcomes

On the best parts of this project, design, PM, and tech were genuinely in discovery together — not design handing off to PM to hand off to eng. The Smart Suggestions feature especially benefited from early engineering involvement in defining what data we could actually use, which shaped the design direction from the start.

Business objective alignment is a design skill

In an org with limited product maturity, I learned that making the business case explicit — connecting design decisions to instructor retention, certification rates, and content engagement — was as important as the design work itself. The journey map became as much a stakeholder alignment tool as a design artifact.
