01

Overview

Les Mills International is a global fitness company behind 16 signature group fitness programs (BODYPUMP, BODYCOMBAT, LES MILLS SPRINT & more) delivered in 20,000+ clubs worldwide. Instructors are the lifeblood of this business: certified, passionate fitness professionals who teach quarterly-updated releases to club members.

The Instructor Experience App was a ground-up redesign of the company's primary instructor-facing digital product, unifying four disconnected platforms into a single, cohesive tool and fundamentally changing how instructors learn, prepare, and teach.

The macro user problem

Instructors struggle with unsustainable prep-to-teaching ratios: mastering quarterly content demands excessive time unaligned with earnings.

Every quarter, Les Mills releases new content for each of its 16 programs. Instructors — most of whom teach part-time alongside day jobs — were expected to learn entirely new choreography, build class playlists, and master updated coaching cues. The time demands were eroding both instructor satisfaction and certification rates.

My Role & Design Approach

As Lead Product Designer I was both the hands-on designer on the Instructor Experience App and concurrently design lead across the whole product org, guiding the team to deliver the end-to-end experience across all digital touchpoints. This meant:

  • Leading user research, journey mapping, and opportunity framing with the Instructor product trio (PM + tech lead) (the company is organized by user base)
  • Designing and prototyping the full app experience: information architecture, interaction design, and visual design
  • Running usability testing and assumption validation in quick iterative cycles, staying in close contact with instructors across different learning styles and life contexts to ensure the final experience served the full spectrum of how people actually learn and teach.
  • Partnering with engineering on feasibility, particularly around the AI/ML components of Smart Suggestions
  • Navigating significant org complexity: designers reported to product directors rather than me, requiring stakeholder alignment work beyond typical IC scope
→

The org context matters for this case study: in addition to hands-on design work on the Instructor App, I was leading the product design chapter across all domains, coaching designers, driving alignment across Movers, Clubs, and Instructor product teams, and doing the upstream work of ensuring cohesive, optimized user journeys end-to-end. Building shared process, embedding research, and creating artifacts like E2E journey maps were as much a part of the role as the design itself.

04

Rather than redesigning the existing Releases App in isolation, we used the E2E instructor journey as our north star — mapping all touchpoints by frequency (weekly, quarterly, bi-annually) and identifying where friction was highest. This surfaced that the highest-frequency activities (learn, prep, teach) lived in the worst product (the aging Releases App).

Case Study 1: Aligning IA to Instructor Expectations

The usability problem (Discovered in first round of usability interviews with beta group)

The app launched with two primary tabs: Learn and Teach. Learn housed the Masterclass video and synced digital choreo notes. Teach housed playlists — the music-only files instructors use to run their own classes.

The problem: the labels created a false binary. Instructors don't learn and teach in neat, separate phases — they move fluidly between modes depending on how they learn. Some are visual learners who watch the Masterclass video. Some are auditory learners who absorb material while driving or training dogs. Some need to read and re-read notes to cement choreography. The tab structure didn't account for any of this.

The result: instructors who wanted audio-only learning couldn't intuitively find it. They expected it to live in Learn — but it lived in Teach, because the music files were technically a playlist. They kept landing in the wrong place.

Learning Hub
Hypothesized instructors would have what they needed to learn new choreo here, then move to the next tab when preparing for teaching.
Teaching Hub
Hypothesized music and schedule to serve instructors while in the teaching phase.

Three approaches considered

1
Show / hide video only
Add a toggle to hide the Masterclass video, leaving just choreo notes and audio. Simple, low dev effort — but didn't solve the mental model problem. Instructors still had to know that "Teach" was where to go for music-only learning.
2
Deep link Learn → Teach
Add a button from the Masterclass screen routing users directly to the playlist. Addressed discoverability but introduced complexity — the video+notes and music+notes were two separate media files that couldn't be perfectly synced, creating a jarring handoff.
3
Reset the IA entirely Chosen + 1
Remove the Learn/Teach designation altogether. Rename the tabs Masterclass, Playlists, and Studio — reflecting what the content is, not how the system expected it to be used. Add show/hide video and voiceover/no-voiceover toggles within Masterclass so learners of every style could configure their experience without leaving the screen.

Decision & rationale

I chose option C combined with A. Options A and B treated symptoms. The real issue was that the existing tab structure imposed a learning sequence that didn't match how instructors actually moved through content. Removing the Learn/Teach designation replaced the cognitive burden of "which tab should I be in?" with a single destination that adapted to each instructor's style.

This was validated through usability testing, where the revised tab labeling and flexible configuration settings significantly reduced navigation errors and time-to-task on first use.

06

Case Study 2: Discovering Smart Suggestions

The user problem

Instructors struggle to efficiently create balanced, varied class playlists from past releases due to a lack of search and organizational tools. This manual, time-consuming process often leads to repetitive classes, reducing member engagement and satisfaction.

Creating a mixed playlist required instructors to manually flip through old PDFs, remember which tracks they'd used recently, and manually sequence a class. 100% of research participants reported losing time to this process — average monthly time spent was 7.5 hours.

How the feature evolved through assumption validation

A
Manual usage tracking

Initial assumption: instructors want to track which tracks they've used. Validation showed this was too much manual overhead — instructors wanted the system to do this work for them.

B
Triangulate schedule + tracks

We explored using teaching schedules cross-referenced with track history to surface gaps. Technically complex and required data we didn't yet have at scale.

C
Ask for higher content frequency Final direction

Surface: instructors wanted content refreshed more frequently than quarterly. This was a product/business constraint — but naming it opened up the smart suggestion framing: surface variety within what exists.

The final Smart Suggestions feature used AI-driven recommendations to propose balanced, varied playlists based on instructor history and class-building best practices — surfacing the right tracks at the right time without requiring manual input.

08

Lessons Learned

E2E journey mapping as a strategic tool

Mapping the full instructor journey — not just the Releases App touchpoints — was the single most important early decision. It reframed the scope from "improve an app" to "redesign a system" and provided shared language for prioritization conversations with stakeholders who had conflicting opinions on where to start.

Assumption validation as a design method, not just a testing method

The Smart Suggestions feature is the clearest example: we had three different hypotheses about what instructors wanted, and each one was partially right. Building small validation experiments — rather than committing to a full build — allowed us to triangulate toward a solution that was meaningfully better than any of the individual hypotheses.

Product trio discovery yields better outcomes

On the best parts of this project, design, PM, and tech were genuinely in discovery together — not design handing off to PM to hand off to eng. The Smart Suggestions feature especially benefited from early engineering involvement in defining what data we could actually use, which shaped the design direction from the start.

Business objective alignment is a design skill

In an org evolving its product practice, I learned that making the business case explicit — connecting design decisions to instructor retention, certification rates, and content engagement — was as important as the design work itself. The journey map became as much a stakeholder alignment tool as a design artifact.

The Problem

Sound Zones is a feature allowing individualized, headset-free audio throughout the cabin. Initial specs and wireframes were handed off from a decoupled team with critical integration gaps. The biggest was a proposed design that would create two separate, conflicting ways for users to select audio sources depending on whether Sound Zones was active. As the owner of the core audio domain, resolving this was mine to solve.

The Solution

Integrated Sound Zones directly into the source drawer as a native extension of source selection, eliminating the duplicate entry point entirely. Replaced manual zone selection with source-driven configuration and latching behavior for a zero-setup experience. Simplified visual controls to maintain spatial context without adding noise. Partnered with UXR to validate the integration approach and overall feature acceptance through in-person clinic testing.

100%
were able to successfully switch the audio source for the passenger upon first try
76%
self-reported willingness to pay a $1,500 premium during in-person clinic testing

A note on outcomes

Despite strong clinic results, Sound Zones was ultimately deprioritised due to shifting business priorities before reaching production. As a result, there are no longitudinal usage metrics to report. I include this project because the design process and problem-solving are worth sharing — and because not every good project ships.
